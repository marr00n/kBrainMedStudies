## Overview of the Thyroid Hormone Synthesis

00:07
The video introduces the synthesis of thyroid hormones and the anatomy of the thyroid gland, which is located in the anterior neck and consists of thyroid follicles, the structural units responsible for hormone production.

01:00
The synthesis of thyroid hormones is initiated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis, where the hypothalamus plays a crucial role by releasing thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH).
## TRH & TSH release
02:04
TRH is secreted by the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus, stimulating the anterior pituitary to release thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH).

## TSH stimulates
03:01
TSH circulates to the thyroid gland, where it binds to receptors on follicular cells, activating transcription factors that leads to the production of thyroglobulin (protein with multiple tyrosines), the precursor for thyroid hormones.

## Iodide trapping
09:07
Iodi<u>de</u> (I-), which is absorbed from the bloodstream through a sodium-linked symporter transporter mechanism, is essential for hormone synthesis. This enables it to carry Iodide up the concentration gradient.

## Iodide oxidation & organification
12:01
The pendrin protein transports iodi<u>de</u> (I<sup>-</sup>) into the follicular cell lumen, where it is **oxidized** to iodi<u>ne</u> (I<sup>2</sup>) through the action of thyroid peroxidase (TPO).

14:01
The iodination process incorporates iodine into tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin (**organification** of iodine), resulting in mono- and di-iodotyrosine molecules (MIT and DIT).

## Coupling and Formation of T3 and T4
16:01
The coupling of iodinated tyrosines yields T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine), which are **collectively** known as thyroid hormones.

## Endocytosis of thyroglobulin
17:57
The final steps involve endocytosis of thyroglobulin containing T3 and T4 back into follicular cells

## Lysosomal enzymes cleave
cleave T3 T4 off thyroglobulin leaving the active hormones
## Exocytosis and Transportation of Thyroid Hormones
19:21
T3 and T4 are then secreted into the bloodstream via exocytosis, where they are transported by thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG) due to their lipophilic nature.
