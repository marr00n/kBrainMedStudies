# Post-operative Infection

## Presenting symptoms and signs

- Increasing pain, swelling, redness, warmth, and tenderness at the surgical site[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Drainage from the wound (either purulent or sero-sanguinous)[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Fever and progressive worsening of inflammatory signs[3](https://www.westernhealth.org.au/Services/Infection_Prevention/Documents/Patient%20info%20Surgical%20site%20infection%20Prevention%20-%20Infection%20Control.pdf)

- Clear distinction from normal post-operative inflammatory signs, which should progressively decrease over time[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)


## How to make the diagnosis

- Presence of bacteria within an inflammatory wound exudate is definitive proof of infection[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Sterile aspiration of the wound or surgical exploration with sample collection[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Microscopic examination (Gram's stain) and appropriate bacteriologic cultures of the exudate[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Tissue specimens should be sampled from 3 or more suspicious sites (one for microbiology and one for histopathology)[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- PCR bacterial identification, if available, is more rapid and reliable than standard cultures[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Serial clinical examinations may be required as local infection must not be mistaken for normal post-operative inflammatory signs[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)

- Early infections may not show radiographic findings until at least 2 weeks after onset[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)


## Management

- Antibiotic therapy tailored to the specific bacteria causing the infection[3](https://www.westernhealth.org.au/Services/Infection_Prevention/Documents/Patient%20info%20Surgical%20site%20infection%20Prevention%20-%20Infection%20Control.pdf)
- Discontinuation of any antibiotic therapy for at least a week prior to tissue sampling for accurate culture results[2](https://surgeryreference.aofoundation.org/orthopedic-trauma/adult-trauma/tibial-shaft/further-reading/diagnosis-of-wound-infection)
- Surgical intervention may be necessary in some cases to adequately treat the infection[3](https://www.westernhealth.org.au/Services/Infection_Prevention/Documents/Patient%20info%20Surgical%20site%20infection%20Prevention%20-%20Infection%20Control.pdf)
- Preventive measures include proper hand hygiene and antiseptic techniques by healthcare providers before surgery[3](https://www.westernhealth.org.au/Services/Infection_Prevention/Documents/Patient%20info%20Surgical%20site%20infection%20Prevention%20-%20Infection%20Control.pdf)

# Post-operative Bleeding

## Presenting symptoms and signs

- Clinical features of hemorrhagic shock: tachycardia, dizziness, agitation, raised respiratory rate, decreased urine output[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- External bleeding from a wound or drain site[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Swelling, discoloration, and disproportionate tenderness at the surgical area[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Peritonism in abdominal cases[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Hemoglobin level decrease of more than 20 g/L within 24 hours[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- For intra-intestinal bleeding: melena, hematemesis, or hematochezia[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- For extra-intestinal bleeding: bloody abdominal drain or abdominal distension[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
## How to make the diagnosis

- Thorough physical examination looking for bleeding sites and associated signs[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Assessment of vital signs with particular attention to the degree of shock[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Blood routine tests to monitor hemoglobin levels[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- Imaging studies including bedside ultrasonography, CT scan, and angiography as appropriate[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/
- Endoscopic evaluation for suspected intra-intestinal bleeding[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- Abdominocentesis in cases of suspected intra-abdominal bleeding[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- Important note: hypotension is often a late sign; do not assume a patient is not bleeding just because their blood pressure is normal[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
## Management

- Initial A to E approach with adequate intravenous access and rapid fluid resuscitation[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Application of direct pressure to visible bleeding sites[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Urgent senior surgical review with appropriate imaging arranged as necessary[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Blood product transfusion (red blood cells, platelets, fresh frozen plasma) in moderate to severe hemorrhage[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Activation of major hemorrhage protocol as needed[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Surgical re-operation for further hemostasis in significant bleeding[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- Conservative management may be appropriate for smaller hemorrhages with close monitoring[4](https://teachmesurgery.com/perioperative/general-complications/haemorrhage/)
- Endoscopic intervention for accessible intra-intestinal bleeding sites[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
- Interventional embolization may be considered for certain cases of extra-intestinal bleeding[5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10007958/)
# Post-operative Venous Thromboembolism (VTE)

## Presenting symptoms and signs

- Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT): leg pain, swelling, warmth, redness, and tenderness
- Pulmonary Embolism (PE): sudden shortness of breath, chest pain (often pleuritic), tachycardia, hypoxemia
- Anxiety or sense of impending doom, particularly with significant pulmonary embolism
- Hemoptysis may occur in some cases of pulmonary embolism[6](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8948856/
## How to make the diagnosis

- Clinical risk assessment using validated tools such as Wells score or Geneva score
- D-dimer testing (high sensitivity but low specificity)
- Ultrasound Doppler of the affected limb for suspected DVT
- CT pulmonary angiogram or V/Q scan for suspected PE
- ECG may show S1Q3T3 pattern or right heart strain in PE
- Chest X-ray is often normal in PE but helps rule out other causes[6](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8948856/)

## Management

- Prophylaxis strategies: early mobilization, mechanical prophylaxis (compression stockings, intermittent pneumatic compression devices)

- Pharmacological prophylaxis with low molecular weight heparin, unfractionated heparin, or direct oral anticoagulants based on risk assessment

- Treatment with therapeutic anticoagulation (initial parenteral followed by oral anticoagulants)

- Thrombolysis, surgical embolectomy, or catheter-directed interventions for severe cases of PE

- IVC filter placement in cases where anticoagulation is contraindicated[6](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8948856/)


# Post-operative Abdominal Sepsis

## Presenting symptoms and signs

- Fever, abdominal pain, and abdominal distension
- Ileus with reduced or absent bowel sounds
- Tachycardia and hypotension in more severe cases
- Altered mental status as sepsis progresses
- Oliguria indicating reduced renal perfusion
- Nausea and vomiting[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)


## How to make the diagnosis

- Clinical examination is often difficult and may not provide definitive diagnosis[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)

- Laboratory tests: elevated white blood cell count, C-reactive protein, and procalcitonin

- Blood cultures to identify causative organisms

- Imaging techniques including CT scan of abdomen and ultrasound are helpful[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)

- Diagnostic laparotomy should be considered early, even in the absence of localizing signs[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)

- Diagnostic paracentesis if ascites is present


## Management

- Aggressive surgical techniques for source control improve prognosis[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)

- Timing of surgical intervention is as critical as the technique used[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)

- Early diagnosis and treatment is particularly crucial in critically ill patients[7](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3319028/)

- Broad-spectrum antibiotics initially, later tailored based on culture results

- Supportive care: fluid resuscitation, vasopressors if needed, respiratory support

- Nutritional support to maintain metabolic function

- Prevention of organ dysfunction through targeted interventions


# Post-operative Respiratory Complications

## Presenting symptoms and signs

- Respiratory complications include: respiratory infection, respiratory failure, pleural effusion, atelectasis, pneumothorax, bronchospasm, and aspiration pneumonitis[8](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10249998/)

- Dyspnea (shortness of breath) and hypoxemia

- Tachypnea and increased work of breathing

- Cough (productive or non-productive)

- Chest pain, particularly with pleural involvement

- Fever in cases of respiratory infection

- Abnormal breath sounds (crackles, wheezes, reduced breath sounds)[8](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10249998/)


## How to make the diagnosis

- Clinical assessment of respiratory function and symptoms

- Oxygen saturation monitoring and arterial blood gas analysis

- Chest X-ray to identify pneumonia, atelectasis, effusions, or pneumothorax

- CT scan of the chest for more detailed evaluation when needed

- Pulmonary function tests to assess respiratory mechanics

- Bronchoscopy in selected cases for diagnosis and therapeutic purposes

- Microbiological studies (sputum culture, blood culture) when infection is suspected[8](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10249998/)


## Management

- Prevention strategies: higher PEEP during surgery, lung protective ventilation strategies[8](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10249998/)

- Oxygen therapy titrated to maintain adequate saturation

- Respiratory physiotherapy and early mobilization to prevent atelectasis

- Incentive spirometry to improve lung expansion

- Bronchodilators for bronchospasm

- Antibiotics for respiratory infections

- Non-invasive ventilation or mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure

- Chest tube placement for pneumothorax

- Thoracentesis for significant pleural effusions[8](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10249998/)


# Post-operative Hypotension

## Presenting symptoms and signs
- Systolic blood pressure (SBP) < 90 mmHg two to 24 hours after surgery[9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38295828/)
- Dizziness and lightheadedness
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Weakness and fatigue
- Pale, clammy skin
- Reduced urine output
- Tachycardia (compensatory mechanism)
- Significantly associated with increased 30-day mortality after surgery[9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38295828/)

## How to make the diagnosis
- Regular and frequent blood pressure monitoring in the post-operative period[9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38295828/)
- Assessment of other vital signs (heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation)
- Clinical evaluation for signs of hypovolemia, cardiac dysfunction, or sepsis
- Laboratory tests: complete blood count, electrolytes, renal function, cardiac enzymes
- ECG to rule out cardiac causes of hypotension
- Echocardiography in selected cases to assess cardiac function
- Assessment of fluid status through clinical and advanced hemodynamic monitoring[9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38295828/)

## Management

- Identification and treatment of the underlying cause (hypovolemia, bleeding, cardiac dysfunction, sepsis, medication effect)

- Fluid resuscitation for hypovolemia

- Blood transfusion for significant bleeding or anemia

- Vasopressors/inotropes if fluid resuscitation is inadequate or contraindicated

- Adjustment or temporary discontinuation of medications that may contribute to hypotension

- Close monitoring of response to interventions

- ICU admission for severe or refractory hypotension[9](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38295828/)


# Post-operative Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

## Presenting symptoms and signs

- Oliguria (urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour) or anuria

- Fluid overload manifesting as edema and pulmonary congestion

- Confusion in severe cases with uremic encephalopathy

- Nausea and vomiting

- Often asymptomatic and detected only through laboratory tests showing elevated creatinine


## How to make the diagnosis

- Monitoring of urine output

- Serial measurements of serum creatinine and calculation of estimated GFR

- Urinalysis and urine microscopy to assess for hematuria, proteinuria, and cellular casts

- Renal ultrasound to rule out obstruction

- Assessment of fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) and urea (FEUrea)

- Classification using established criteria such as RIFLE, AKIN, or KDIGO


## Management

- Identification and treatment of the underlying cause

- Discontinuation of nephrotoxic medications

- Optimization of fluid status (correction of hypovolemia while avoiding fluid overload)

- Maintenance of adequate mean arterial pressure to ensure renal perfusion

- Close monitoring of fluid balance, electrolytes, and acid-base status

- Renal replacement therapy (dialysis) in severe cases

- Nutritional support with appropriate protein intake

- Adjustment of medication dosages according to renal function

- Nephrology consultation for complex cases
