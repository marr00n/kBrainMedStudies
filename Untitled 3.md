Intravenous anaesthetic agents such as propofol are commonly used to induce general anaesthesia, while volatile anaesthetic agents are the most commonly used drugs for maintenance of anaesthesia.

---

Inhalational agents are administered according to a specific concentration, expressed as a percentage of the volume of anaesthetic gas to the total volume of the gas mixture.

---

Modern anaesthetic vaporisers are able to vaporise liquid inhalational anaesthetic agents such that very accurate concentrations can be delivered to the patient.

---

The inhalational agents currently in clinical use are sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane, and nitrous oxide (N₂O). Sevoflurane, desflurane, and isoflurane are synthetic, colourless, non-flammable liquids administered as a vapour from a vaporiser on the anaesthesia machine.

---

The exact mechanism of action of inhalational anaesthetic agents is likely multifactorial.

---

Three proposed mechanisms of action for volatile anaesthetics are: (1) a volume expansion theory (the Mullins critical volume hypothesis), whereby the agents dissolve into lipid cell membranes in the CNS and may distort sodium channels required for synaptic transmission; (2) neurotransmitter interference, whereby the agents may act at presynaptic and postsynaptic receptors, inhibiting release, reuptake, or postsynaptic binding and thereby altering synaptic transmission; and (3) activation of GABA receptors and inhibition of calcium channels and/or glutamate channels, resulting in inhibition of neurotransmitter release in the CNS.

---

The MAC value of an inhalational agent is the alveolar concentration in oxygen at one atmosphere of pressure that prevents 50% of subjects from making a purposeful movement in response to a painful stimulus such as a surgical incision.

---

Without other medications, an anaesthetic depth equivalent to 1.2–1.3 MAC is required for surgical procedures. This added 20–30% above 1 MAC will prevent movement in 95% of patients.

---

The rapidity with which the anaesthetic state is reached depends on how quickly the inhalational agent reaches the patient's brain to exert its partial pressure effects.

---

Factors determining how quickly the inhalational agent reaches the alveoli include: (1) the inspired concentration of anaesthetic gas being delivered by the anaesthetic machine (the concentration effect); (2) the gas flow rate through the anaesthetic machine; and (3) alveolar ventilation (V̇A), where V̇A = respiratory rate × tidal volume.

---

Factors determining how quickly the inhalational agent reaches the brain from the alveoli include: (1) the rate of blood flow to the brain; (2) the solubility of the inhalational agent in the brain; and (3) the difference in the arterial and venous concentrations of the inhalational agent.

---

The F_A/F_I ratio describes the relationship between the alveolar concentration (F_A) and the inspired concentration (F_I). F_I is the constant concentration delivered by the machine; F_A is the variable concentration that builds up in the alveolus, which tracks the brain concentration because the alveolus, arterial blood, and brain rapidly equilibrate. A highly soluble agent causes blood to drain the alveolus, keeping F_A low relative to F_I and producing a low, slow-rising curve. An insoluble agent is taken up poorly by blood, allowing F_A to build up quickly and producing a high, steep curve.

---

Patients in low cardiac output states such as shock may experience a rapid rise in the alveolar partial pressure of an inhalational anaesthetic agent, resulting in a more rapid onset of anaesthesia with possible exaggerated cardiorespiratory depressant effects. Low cardiac output reduces blood uptake from the alveolus, raising F_A and therefore raising the arterial partial pressure and the partial pressure gradient into the brain. Deeper anaesthesia then produces further cardiorespiratory depression — since volatile agents are negative inotropes and vasodilators — which reduces cardiac output further, raising F_A further still in a self-perpetuating cycle.

---

The cascade of anaesthetic partial pressure starts at the vaporiser. With a circle system, a fresh gas flow of 4–5 L·min⁻¹ will raise the inspired anaesthetic tension close to the vaporiser's delivered concentration. As the body continues to take up the inhaled anaesthetic gas, the alveolar anaesthetic tension will remain below the inspired anaesthetic tension for many hours. The brain is the final step in the anaesthetic cascade, and brain tension will approach alveolar tension within 8–10 minutes of any change. Monitoring the alveolar end-tidal concentration of the inhaled anaesthetic therefore provides a reliable estimate of brain concentration. The terms tension and concentration are often used interchangeably in this context; both are more precisely described as partial pressure.

---

Isoflurane has a MAC value of 1.16%. It has a pungent odour and a tendency to irritate the airway, causing patients to cough and hold their breath when administered too quickly. For this reason, isoflurane is generally not used to induce anaesthesia.

---

Isoflurane is useful for neurosurgical procedures as it reduces cerebral metabolic rate and causes minimal change in cerebral blood flow and intracranial pressure.

---

Isoflurane has minimal depressant effects on the myocardium. The reduction in blood pressure it produces is accompanied by a similar reduction in vascular resistance, such that there is little change in cardiac output.

---

Sevoflurane does not have an unpleasant smell and is well tolerated for inhalational induction of anaesthesia. With high inspired concentrations of 5–8%, a patient can be taken from an awake state to a sleep state with as little as one or two vital capacity breaths. An anaesthetic depth sufficient to permit laryngeal mask placement and tracheal intubation can be achieved after three to five minutes of breathing a high concentration of sevoflurane.

---

Sevoflurane produces a dose-related reduction in blood pressure comparable with isoflurane and desflurane. This occurs due to a decrease in systemic vascular resistance without a corresponding decrease in cardiac output.

---

Sevoflurane is a potent bronchodilator and has been used in the treatment of patients with status asthmaticus who are resistant to medical therapy and require controlled ventilation.

---

Sevoflurane, isoflurane, and desflurane all produce uterine relaxation in a dose-dependent manner.

---

Postoperative nausea and vomiting after sevoflurane is comparable with that after isoflurane and desflurane, but greater than that associated with a total intravenous anaesthetic technique using propofol.

---

Sevoflurane degrades to compound A in the presence of a carbon dioxide absorber.

---

Desflurane has the most rapid onset and offset of anaesthetic effect compared with sevoflurane or isoflurane, and it offers the shortest time to emergence and orientation following anaesthesia.

---

Desflurane is the most pungent of the inhalational anaesthetics and is not suitable for inhalational induction due to its irritating properties.

---

A marked activation of the sympathetic nervous system may occur when desflurane concentrations are increased rapidly or high concentrations are used. This manifests as tachycardia and hypertension — and occasionally myocardial ischaemia — and is associated with a surge in plasma norepinephrine, epinephrine, and antidiuretic hormone (ADH) levels.

---

The Rule of 24 states that the risk of an adverse sympathetic surge associated with desflurane is reduced when the product of the carrier gas flow rate (fresh gas flow in L/min) multiplied by the inspired concentration of desflurane (%) is less than 24. This product captures how rapidly desflurane is being delivered into the circuit: a high flow rate combined with a high inspired concentration results in rapid wash-in and sympathetic surge risk.

---

In the presence of dry, desiccated CO₂ absorbers, desflurane — and to a lesser extent isoflurane and sevoflurane — can degrade to form carbon monoxide (CO). Carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) poisoning has been reported following CO₂ absorber desiccation in the presence of desflurane.

---

Nitrous oxide is an inert, inorganic, colourless, tasteless, and odourless gas. It has a rapid onset and a quick recovery of 3–10 minutes due to its low solubility in blood.

---

Nitrous oxide has a MAC of 104%, meaning it cannot produce surgical anaesthesia as a sole agent at atmospheric pressure. Its low potency limits the amount that can be administered and its usefulness when high concentrations of oxygen are required.

---

Nitrous oxide is 34 times more soluble than nitrogen. At the beginning of anaesthesia, N₂O leaves the alveoli much faster than nitrogen can leave the body tissues to fill the space left by N₂O. The result is an increase in the concentration of the remaining alveolar gases — oxygen and other inhalational agents. This increase in concentration speeds the onset of inhalational anaesthetic effect and is referred to as the second gas effect.

---

Diffusion hypoxia may result at the end of an anaesthetic involving nitrous oxide. As N₂O is discontinued, body stores of nitrous oxide are released and flood the alveoli, diluting the oxygen present. When only room air is administered at the end of the anaesthetic, this dilution may be sufficient to create a hypoxic mixture and result in hypoxaemia. Other contributing factors at emergence include respiratory depression from anaesthetic agents, residual neuromuscular blockade, and pain with splinted respirations. Administration of 100% oxygen at the end of the anaesthetic may avoid hypoxaemia resulting from any of these causes.

---

Closed air spaces will expand in the presence of nitrous oxide due to the difference in solubility between nitrogen and nitrous oxide. With the administration of 66% N₂O, a closed air space will expand twofold in volume over approximately 15 minutes. Nitrous oxide is therefore contraindicated in patients with a pneumothorax, closed-loop bowel obstruction, air embolism, or any other closed air space in the body.

---

As the recommended minimum concentration of oxygen delivered during general anaesthesia is 30%, the maximum concentration of N₂O that can be used is 70%, which equates to approximately 0.7 MAC.

---

Sevoflurane, desflurane, and isoflurane are all contraindicated in patients with malignant hyperthermia.

---

Opioid administration decreases volatile MAC levels by 50–70%. This is because MAC is defined by a spinal reflex endpoint — movement in response to a surgical stimulus — rather than a cortical one. Opioids act at mu receptors in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, blunting the nociceptive afferent signal that feeds that reflex arc. By suppressing the same endpoint via a different mechanism and site, opioids reduce the amount of volatile agent needed to suppress the residual signal.

---

The Meyer-Overton Rule states that the anaesthetic potency of a gas is directly proportional to its lipid (oil) solubility. A higher oil:gas partition coefficient indicates higher lipid solubility, which confers higher potency and therefore a lower MAC.

---

Blood:gas solubility governs the speed of onset and offset of an inhalational anaesthetic agent.
