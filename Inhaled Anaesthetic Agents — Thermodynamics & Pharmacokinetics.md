The clinical effect of an inhaled agent is determined by its partial pressure (PP) at the effect site (brain). Understanding uptake and distribution requires a grasp of three foundational physical concepts: PP, concentration, and solubility. These are distinct but related quantities, and confusing them leads to confusion about induction speed, the second gas effect, and compartment kinetics.

## Foundational Physical Concepts

### Pressure and Partial Pressure
- Pressure is generated by gas molecules in constant random motion colliding with surfaces — more molecules or faster molecules → ↑ pressure.
- In a gas mixture, each constituent exerts its own PP independently, proportional to its fractional concentration (Dalton's law). Molecules of one gas are indifferent to those of another.
- PP of a gas = fractional concentration × total pressure. At sea level (~100kPa): 2% sevoflurane → PP ~2kPa.
- In the alveolus, total pressure is fixed at atmospheric. ∴ ↑ fractional concentration → ↑ PP proportionally.

### Concentration vs Partial Pressure
- **Concentration** — amount of substance per unit volume. Describes how much is present; says nothing about its tendency to move.
- **Partial pressure** — the thermodynamic activity (escaping tendency) of a dissolved gas. Determines the drive for net movement between compartments.
- Net movement of a gas occurs from high PP → low PP, regardless of concentration gradient. Movement ceases when PP equalises on both sides — even if concentrations differ markedly.
- Analogy: heat flows from high temperature → low temperature until temperatures equalise, not until heat content is equal. PP is to gas as temperature is to heat.

### Solubility and the Partition Coefficient
- **Solubility** describes the capacity of a medium to hold a gas at a given PP. High solubility = large amounts dissolve without generating much PP.
- **Partition coefficient** is the quantitative expression of solubility: the ratio of concentrations in two phases at equilibrium (equal PP). The blood:gas partition coefficient (λ) answers: at equal PP, how many more molecules are in blood than in gas?
    - λ = 2.0 → blood holds twice the concentration of agent as alveolar gas at equal PP.
    - λ = 0.47 (N₂O) → blood holds less than half the concentration as alveolar gas.
> [!Hint]
> The blood:gas partition coefficient is the quantitative measure of the solubility of the agent in blood relative to alveolar gas. It answers the question: at equilibrium, for every molecule in the alveolar gas phase, how many are dissolved in blood?
> 
> So when someone says an agent has high solubility in blood, they mean the blood:gas coefficient is high — the two statements are interchangeable.
> 
> The reason both terms exist is that "solubility" is the conceptual description and the partition coefficient is the numerical expression of it. Solubility tells you the principle; the partition coefficient gives you the number.

- **Henry's law**: at constant temperature, the concentration of a dissolved gas in a liquid is directly proportional to its PP above the liquid. The proportionality constant (Henry's law constant) is specific to each gas–liquid pair and changes with temperature. It is functionally equivalent to solubility.
- Solubility is analogous to thermal mass (specific heat capacity): a medium with high solubility absorbs large amounts of gas before its PP rises, just as a substance with high thermal mass absorbs large amounts of heat before its temperature rises.

### Diffusion Across Membranes (Fick's Law)
- Gases cross membranes (including the blood–brain barrier) by dissolving into the lipid bilayer, traversing it, and dissolving out the other side.
- Net flux is proportional to the PP gradient across the membrane — not the concentration gradient.
- Equilibrium is reached when PP equalises on both sides. At this point concentrations may differ substantially, determined by the relative solubilities of each compartment.
- The brain equilibrates to the PP presented by arterial blood. It responds to the PP signal delivered — not to the absolute amount of agent in blood.

## Pharmacokinetics of Inhaled Agents

### The FA/FI Ratio
- **FA** = fractional alveolar concentration. **FI** = fractional inspired concentration.
- FA/FI tracks the rise of alveolar PP toward inspired PP. Ratio of 1.0 = full equilibration.
- ↑ FA/FI → ↑ alveolar PP → ↑ arterial PP → ↑ brain PP → faster induction.

### Step 1 — Inspired Gas to Alveolus
Factors that ↑ FA:
- ↑ FI (inspired concentration) — directly ↑ alveolar PP.
- ↑ Alveolar ventilation — replenishes agent removed by uptake; VA/FRC ratio is the key variable.
- ↓ FRC — ↓ dilution of inspired agent per breath.
- ↓ Circuit volume and ↓ circuit absorption — ↑ FI delivered to patient.

### Step 2 — Alveolus to Blood (Uptake)
Uptake is determined by three factors:
**1. Blood:gas partition coefficient (λ)**
- Low λ (e.g. desflurane 0.45, sevoflurane 0.65) → blood has low capacity → few molecules required to raise blood PP to alveolar PP → rapid equilibration → blood leaving alveolus carries high PP signal → fast induction.
- High λ (e.g. isoflurane 1.4, halothane 2.4) → blood has large capacity → many molecules dissolve before blood PP rises → blood leaves alveolus at low PP relative to alveolus → attenuated PP signal to brain → slow induction.
- Low solubility = faithful transmission of alveolar PP to brain. High solubility = attenuated transmission.

**2. Cardiac output (alveolar blood flow)**
- ↑ CO → ↑ blood flow past alveolus → ↑ uptake → alveolus drained more aggressively → FA suppressed → slower rise of FA/FI → slower induction.
- ↓ CO (e.g. shock, cardiac failure) → ↓ alveolar drainage → FA rises faster → deeper anaesthesia than expected at a given vaporiser setting → risk of overdose, particularly with soluble agents.
- Effect more pronounced with high-λ agents, where uptake is already large.

**3. Alveolar–venous PP gradient (PA − Pv)**
- Pv (mixed venous PP) reflects tissue saturation. Early in induction: all tissues unsaturated → avid extraction → Pv low → large PA − Pv gradient → ↑ uptake → FA suppressed → slow induction.
- As tissues saturate: Pv ↑ → gradient narrows → uptake ↓ → FA rises more freely.
- Large AV gradient = tissues still hungry for agent = slow induction. Narrow AV gradient = tissues approaching saturation = faster rise of FA.

### Step 3 — Blood to Tissues (Distribution)
Tissue uptake is determined by: tissue blood flow, tissue:blood partition coefficient, and arterial PP − tissue PP gradient.

Tissues are grouped by equilibration time:

| Group             | Tissues                      | Blood Flow | Equilibration         |
|-------------------|------------------------------|------------|-----------------------|
| Vessel-rich (VRG) | Brain, heart, liver, kidneys | High       | Minutes               |
| Muscle            | Skeletal muscle              | Moderate   | Hours                 |
| Fat               | Adipose tissue               | Low        | Days                  |
| Vessel-poor       | Bone, connective tissue      | Negligible | Clinically irrelevant |

- At induction, all tissues have zero agent → maximal extraction → venous blood returning to lungs is depleted → FA suppressed.
- VRG equilibrates first (minutes). Brain PP rises to equal arterial PP → anaesthesia established. VRG then ceases to act as a sink.
- Muscle becomes the dominant ongoing sink over hours. Fat accumulates agent slowly but becomes a large reservoir in prolonged anaesthesia → relevant for emergence after long cases with soluble agents.
- As tissues saturate: Pv ↑ → AV gradient ↓ → less uptake per breath → FA/FI climbs → maintenance requires less agent over time.
- Emergence after short cases is fast (muscle and fat have absorbed little → rapid washout). After prolonged cases, muscle acts as a reservoir → delayed emergence.
- This tissue grouping is the physiological basis of the three-compartment pharmacokinetic model used in TIVA: central compartment = VRG; peripheral compartment 1 = muscle; peripheral compartment 2 = fat. The same framework applies to inhaled agents.
## The Concentration Effect and Second Gas Effect
- When N₂O is co-administered with a volatile agent, it ↑ the rate of uptake of the volatile — the **second gas effect**.
- N₂O (λ = 0.47) is given at high FI (~70%) and is taken up rapidly in large bulk volumes 2° to its low λ (rapid equilibration) and high delivered concentration.
- Bulk absorption of N₂O → alveolar volume shrinks → fractional concentration of remaining gases (including sevoflurane) ↑ → PP of sevoflurane ↑ → ↑ uptake gradient → faster induction. This is the **concentration effect** — the mechanism underlying the second gas effect.
- Secondary mechanism: alveolar shrinkage draws fresh gas in from dead space → further replenishment of volatile agent in alveolus.
- The mechanism is ↑ fractional concentration (and therefore PP) of the second gas — not N₂O consuming blood's capacity for the volatile agent.
- **Diffusion hypoxia** at emergence is the same phenomenon in reverse: N₂O leaves blood rapidly in bulk → dilutes alveolar O₂ → ↓ PAO₂ → hypoxaemia. Prevent with 100% O₂ at end of case.
## MAC Additivity vs Dalton's Law Additivity
- **Dalton's law additivity** (physical): in a gas mixture, each gas exerts its own PP independently. Gases do not interfere with each other's PP. Total pressure = sum of individual PPs.
- **MAC additivity** (pharmacodynamic): 0.5 MAC N₂O + 0.5 MAC sevoflurane = 1.0 MAC clinical effect. The brain integrates the individual PP contributions of each agent to produce a summed effect on consciousness and movement suppression.
- These are additive at different levels: Dalton's law is a physical property of gas mixtures; MAC additivity is an observed property of CNS depression.
- The combined PP of two agents does not constitute a single unified PP that has physical meaning — each agent acts on the brain according to its own PP independently.
