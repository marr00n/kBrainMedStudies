> Oximetry is based on the differences in light absorption by haemoglobin as it binds and releases oxygen. Red and infrared light frequencies are transmitted through a translucent portion of tissue, such as the fingertip or earlobe. The signal is filtered to isolate pulsatile changes in light absorption. Microprocessors are then used to analyse the amount of light absorbed by two wavelengths of light, and this is compared with a table of measured values to determine the concentration of oxygenated and deoxygenated forms of haemoglobin. Once the concentrations of oxyhaemoglobin (oxyHb) and deoxyhaemoglobin (deoxyHb) have been determined, the oxygen saturation (SpO2) can be calculated.

>
> Pulse oximetry includes measurements of oxyHb, deoxyHb, methaemoglobin (metHb), and carboxyhaemoglobin (carboxyHb).

> A pulmonary artery catheter (PAC) passes through the right atrium and right ventricle and rests in a branch of one of the pulmonary arteries. Inflation of a small balloon at the tip of the catheter allows occlusion of the proximal pulmonary artery and measurement of the distal pressure. This distal (back) pressure is referred to as the pulmonary artery (or capillary) wedge pressure (PCWP) or pulmonary artery occlusion pressure (PAOP) and reflects the left atrial filling pressure.

> Bispectral Index (BIS™) monitor analyses the electroencephalogram (EEG) signal. A complex algorithm processes the EEG signal and provides the clinician with a single number as a measure of anaesthetic depth. The value reported ranges from 0 = a silent EEG to 100 = fully awake. The manufacturer (Aspect Medical) recommends a value of 40–60 as an appropriate level for general anaesthesia.
