Volatile agents (sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane) are synthetic, colourless, non-flammable liquids administered as vapour via calibrated vaporisers. They are the most widely used agents for maintenance of GA. 

Nitrous oxide (N2O) is a separate inorganic gas, administered as a supplement. All act by poorly understood, likely multifactorial mechanisms; potency correlates with lipid solubility (Meyer-Overton rule).

## Pharmacokinetic Principles

### MAC

• Minimum alveolar concentration (MAC) = alveolar concentration (in O2, at 1 atmosphere) preventing purposeful movement in response to surgical stimulus in 50% of subjects.
• 1.2–1.3 MAC required for surgery without adjuncts → prevents movement in ~95% of patients.
• MAC is a spinal reflex endpoint (dorsal horn), not a cortical one → explains why opioids are MAC-sparing.
• Opioids ↓ MAC by 50–70% via mu-receptor action at the dorsal horn, blunting the nociceptive afferent arc.

### FA/FI Ratio & Uptake

• F<sub>I</sub> = inspired fraction (set at the vaporiser); F<sub>A</sub> = alveolar fraction (variable).
• F<sub>A</sub> tracks brain concentration: alveolus ⇌ arterial blood ⇌ brain — rapid equilibration assumed.
• **Soluble agent** (high blood:gas coefficient) → blood drains the alveolus → F<sub>A</sub> stays low → slow onset (low F<sub>A</sub>/F<sub>I</sub> curve).
• **Insoluble agent** (low blood:gas coefficient) → blood leaves it → F<sub>A</sub> builds quickly → fast onset (high F<sub>A</sub>/F<sub>I</sub> curve).
• Blood:gas solubility governs speed of onset and offset.

### Anaesthetic Tension Cascade

• Vaporiser → inspired gas (diluted by exhaled gas in circuit) → alveolus → arterial blood → brain.
• FGF 4–5 L/min with a circle system raises inspired concentration close to vaporiser setting.
• Alveolar tension remains below inspired tension for many hours (ongoing uptake by blood).
• Brain tension approaches alveolar tension within 8–10min of any change.
• End-tidal concentration is therefore a reliable surrogate for brain concentration.

### Low Cardiac Output State

• ↓ CO → less blood draining the alveolus → ↑ F<sub>A</sub> → ↑ arterial partial pressure → deeper anaesthesia than expected at a given vaporiser setting.
• Vicious cycle: deeper anaesthesia → ↑ cardiovascular depression (negative inotropy + vasodilation) → ↓ CO further → ↑ F<sub>A</sub> → deeper still.
• Reduce vaporiser setting proactively in shocked or low-CO patients.

## Individual Agents

### Isoflurane

- **MAC:** 1.16%
- **Airway:** Pungent odour; airway irritant → coughing and breath-holding with rapid administration. Not suitable for inhalational induction.
- **CVS:** ↓ BP 2° to ↓ SVR; cardiac output relatively preserved. Minimal myocardial depressant effect.
- **CNS:** ↓ cerebral metabolic rate; minimal change in cerebral blood flow or ICP → useful in neurosurgery.
- **Uterus:** Dose-dependent uterine relaxation (see also sevoflurane, desflurane).
- **CI:** Malignant hyperthermia (MH) susceptibility.

### Sevoflurane

- **MAC:** ~2% (not pungent; well tolerated for inhalational induction).
- **Airway:** Pleasant smell; minimal irritation. Preferred agent for gas induction.
  - Inhalational induction: 5–8% inspired concentration → sleep in 1–2 vital capacity breaths.
  - Depth for SGA/TT placement achievable within 3–5min.
- **CVS:** Dose-related ↓ BP comparable to isoflurane and desflurane; mechanism is ↓ SVR without corresponding ↓ CO.
- **Respiratory:** Potent bronchodilator — has been used in refractory status asthmaticus requiring controlled ventilation.
- **Uterus:** Dose-dependent uterine relaxation.
- **PONV:** Comparable with isoflurane and desflurane; greater than TIVA with propofol.
- **Degradation:** Metabolised to Compound A in the presence of CO2 absorbers — potential renal tubular toxicity at high concentrations/low FGF (clinical significance debated; avoid prolonged low-flow sevoflurane).
- **CI:** MH susceptibility.

### Desflurane

- **Onset/offset:** Most rapid of the volatiles → shortest time to emergence and orientation.
- **Airway:** Most pungent of all volatile agents; **not suitable for inhalational induction**.
- **CVS:** Dose-related ↓ BP via ↓ SVR.
  - Rapid ↑ in desflurane concentration or use of high concentrations → sympathetic activation → ↑ HR, ↑ BP ± myocardial ischaemia (↑ plasma noradrenaline, adrenaline, ADH).
  - **Rule of 24:** FGF (L/min) × inspired desflurane concentration (%) must be **< 24** to avoid sympathetic surge.
- **Uterus:** Dose-dependent uterine relaxation.
- **Degradation:** Desiccated CO2 absorbers → CO production; carboxyhaemoglobin poisoning reported. Risk greatest with desflurane; lesser with isoflurane and sevoflurane.
- **CI:** MH susceptibility.

### Nitrous Oxide (N2O)

- **Properties:** Inert, inorganic, colourless, odourless, tasteless gas.
- **MAC:** 104% → cannot produce GA alone at safe concentrations.
- **Onset/offset:** Rapid — recovery 3–10min; due to low blood solubility.
- **Max usable concentration:** 70% (minimum delivered FiO2 during GA = 30% → max N2O = 70%, providing ~0.7 MAC contribution).

#### Second Gas Effect
• N2O is 34× more soluble in blood than nitrogen.
• On induction: N2O rapidly leaves the alveolus into blood → N2 cannot leave tissues quickly enough to fill the space → residual alveolar gases (O2 and co-administered volatiles) become concentrated → ↑ FA of co-agent → faster onset of inhalational effect.

#### Diffusion Hypoxia
• On discontinuation: body stores of N2O flood back into alveoli → dilutes alveolar O2 → risk of hypoxaemia.
• Administer 100% O2 at end of anaesthesia to prevent diffusion hypoxia (also guards against residual NMB, respiratory depression, and pain-related splinting).

#### Closed Air Space Expansion
• N2O diffuses into closed spaces faster than N2 diffuses out → volume expansion.
• 66% N2O → 2× expansion of a closed air space over ~15min.
• **CI:** Pneumothorax, closed loop bowel obstruction, air embolism, intracranial air, middle ear/sinus surgery (relative), vitreoretinal gas tamponade.

## Special Hazards & Cross-Cutting Notes

| Hazard | Agents | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Malignant hyperthermia | Isoflurane, sevoflurane, desflurane | All volatile halogenated agents are CI in MH-susceptible patients; use TIVA |
| CO production | Desflurane >> isoflurane > sevoflurane | 2° to desiccated CO2 absorbers; monitor COHb if suspected |
| Compound A | Sevoflurane | Formed with CO2 absorbers at low FGF; avoid prolonged low-flow sevoflurane |
| Uterine relaxation | Isoflurane, sevoflurane, desflurane | Dose-dependent; relevant in obstetrics and uterine surgery |
| MAC-sparing | All volatiles | Opioids ↓ MAC 50–70%; N2O contributes additively to MAC |
