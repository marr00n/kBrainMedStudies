## Constants at Rest (not Inspiring or expiring)
- Atmospheric pressure = 760mmHg
- Intra-pulmonary pressure (P<sub>pul​</sub>) = 760mmHg (net 0 from atm)
- Intra-pleural pressure = 756 (-4mmHg)

### Why is Intra-pleural Negative?
- Elasticity of lungs (pulls parietal pleura away from chest wall)
- Surface tension (pulls parietal pleura away from chest wall)
- Elasticity of chest wall (pulls visceral pleura away from lungs)
- Gravity (this means that intra-pleural pressure inferiorly is higher than superiorly)
> Together these lead to increased intra-pleural volume, and by Boyle's Law makes the pressure negative.

## Trans-wall Pressure Gradients
- Transpulmonary Pressure (btwn intra-pulmonary and pleural space) = -4mmHg gradient
- <b>Transpulmonary Pressure (P<sub>pul</sub>​−P<sub>ip</sub>​):</b> The difference across the lung wall (approx. **+4 mmHg**). A positive value is essential to keep the lungs inflated.
- Transthoracic Pressure (between pleural space and atmosphere) = -4mmHg gradient too
- <b>Transthoracic Pressure (P<sub>ip​</sub>−P<sub>atm​</sub>):</b> The difference across the chest wall (approx. **-4 mmHg**). This acts as a deflating pressure.

## Inspiration Mechanics

### 1. Neural Regulation

- **Control Centres:**
	- Voluntary control by by the **cerebral cortex**
	- Involuntary control by [[ventral respiratory group (VRG)]] in the [[medulla]].
- **Nerve Pathways:**
    - **[[Phrenic Nerve]]** (C3-C5): Innervates the [[diaphragm]].
    - **[[Intercostal Nerves]]** (T1-T11): Innervate the [[external intercostal muscles]].

### 2. Muscular Action & Volume Changes

- **Diaphragm:** Contracts and "domes down" (depresses), significantly increasing the vertical volume of the thoracic cavity.
- **External Intercostals:** Pull the ribs outward (**"bucket handle" movement**) and push the sternum forward (**"pump handle action"**), increasing the horizontal and anterior-posterior volume.
- **Overall Effect:** The expansion of the thoracic wall pulls the **parietal pleura** outward; because the pleural space is a potential space, this pulls the **visceral pleura** and the lungs with it, increasing lung volume.

### 3. Pressure Transitions (Boyle’s Law)

In accordance with **Boyle’s Law**, as the volume of the thoracic cavity increases, the internal pressures decrease:

- **Intrapleural Pressure ($P_{ip}$):** Becomes more negative, dropping from -4 mmHg at rest to approximately **-6 mmHg**.
- **Intrapulmonary Pressure ($P_{pul}$):** Drops from 0 mmHg to approximately **-1 or -2 mmHg** (relative to the atmosphere).
- **Transpulmonary Pressure:** The difference between $P_{pul}$ and $P_{ip}$ increases (e.g. to **+5 mmHg**), which is the force that keeps the lungs open during inspiration.
- **Trans-respiratory Pressure:** Becomes negative (approx. **-1 mmHg**), indicating the total gradient is directed into the lungs.

### 4. Gas Flow & Equilibrium

- **Pressure Gradient:** Following the **law of diffusion**, air moves from areas of high pressure (atmosphere at 760 mmHg) to low pressure (alveoli at ~759 mmHg).
- **Equilibrium:** Air continues to flow into the lungs until the intrapulmonary pressure reaches **equilibrium** with the atmosphere (0 mmHg) at the **peak of inspiration**.

## Mechanics of Breathing: Expiration

### 1. Forced Inspiration: Accessory Muscles
While quiet inspiration relies on the diaphragm and external intercostals, **forced inspiration** (e.g., during heavy lifting or exercise) recruits accessory muscles to further increase thoracic cavity volume:
-   Sternocleidomastoid (SCM): Elevates and pulls the sternum outwards.
-   Scalenes (Anterior, Middle, Posterior): Pull the ribs upwards.
-   Pectoralis Minor: Connects to the 3rd, 4th, and 5th ribs to assist in pulling them up.
-   **Impact on Pressure:** These muscles cause a greater increase in volume, dropping **intrapleural pressure ($P_{ip}$)** to approximately **-7 mmHg** and **intrapulmonary pressure ($P_{pul}$)** to **-2 mmHg (758 mmHg)**. This deeper pressure gradient allows significantly more air to flow into the lungs.

### 2. Quiet Expiration: A Passive Process
Unlike inspiration, **quiet expiration is entirely passive** and involves no muscle contraction.
-   **The Stretch Reflex:** During inspiration, **stretch receptors** in the bronchi and lungs detect the expansion and send inhibitory signals to the **Ventral Respiratory Group (VRG)** in the medulla.
-   **Neural Inhibition:** This shuts down the nerve impulses travelling through the **phrenic nerve** (to the diaphragm) and **intercostal nerves** (to the external intercostals).
-   **Muscle Relaxation:** The diaphragm relaxes and domes upwards, while the ribs and sternum recoil downwards and inwards.
-   **Lung Elasticity:** The natural **elasticity** of the lungs drives the reduction in lung volume.
-   **Boyle’s Law:** As volume decreases, internal pressure must increase. $P_{ip}$ returns to **-4 mmHg**, and $P_{pul}$ rises to **+1 mmHg (761 mmHg)**, pushing air out into the atmosphere until equilibrium (0 mmHg/760 mmHg) is reached.

### 3. Forced Expiration: An Active Process
When extra effort is needed to exhale (e.g., coughing or exercise), the process becomes **active** and requires specific muscles:
-   Internal Intercostals: These muscles pull the upper ribs downwards, depressing the rib cage and pushing the sternum inwards to rapidly decrease thoracic volume.
-   Abdominal Wall Muscles: Includes the external/internal obliques, transverse abdominis, and rectus abdominis.
-   **The Abdominal Mechanism:** Contraction of these muscles increases **intra-abdominal pressure**, which forcefully pushes the diaphragm upwards into the thoracic cavity.
-   **Pressure Dynamics:** Forced expiration decreases thoracic volume so significantly that $P_{pul}$ may rise to **+2 mmHg (762 mmHg)** and $P_{ip}$ to **-3 mmHg**. This creates a steeper pressure gradient (2 mmHg vs 1 mmHg), forcing air out of the lungs more rapidly.
