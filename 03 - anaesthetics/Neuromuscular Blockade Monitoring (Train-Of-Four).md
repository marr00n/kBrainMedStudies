Peripheral nerve stimulation provides an objective assessment of neuromuscular transmission in patients receiving NMBA infusions. Electrical stimulation of a peripheral nerve → muscle contraction proportional to available (unblocked) nicotinic ACh receptors. Used to titrate NMBA dose and avoid under- or over-paralysis.

> [!important]
> • Excessive/prolonged paralysis → ICU-acquired weakness → delayed recovery  
> • Use lowest NMBA dose achieving clinical goal

---

### Principles

• Electrical stimulus depolarises peripheral nerve → ACh release → muscle contraction  
• NMBA block nicotinic receptors → ↓ number of activated fibres → ↓ contraction strength  
• Objective assessment avoids clinical guesswork in sedated/paralysed patients  

---

### Indications

• Continuous NMBA infusion (ICU, ARDS, severe ventilator dyssynchrony)  
• Assessment of depth of neuromuscular blockade  
• Titration of NMBA dose  

---

### Technique (Train-of-four, TOF)

• Delivers **4 electrical stimuli** over ~**1.5sec**  
  • Interval ~**0.5sec** between stimuli  
  • Pulse duration ~**0.2ms**  

• Assess:
  • Number of twitches (0–4)  
  • Strength and fade (T1 → T4)  

---

### Stimulation sites

#### Ulnar nerve (preferred)

• Location: wrist (ulnar side)  
• Muscle: **adductor pollicis** → thumb adduction  
• Advantages:
  • Reliable nerve stimulation (↓ risk of direct muscle stimulation)  
  • Widely used standard site  

• Electrode placement:
  • Distal (black, −): over nerve at wrist crease  
  • Proximal (red, +): ≥2cm proximal  

---

#### Facial nerve

• Branch: temporal branch  
• Muscle: **corrugator supercilii** → eyebrow movement  

• Electrode placement:
  • Distal (−): lateral canthus  
  • Proximal (+): ~2cm inferior, near tragus  

> [!note]
> More susceptible to direct muscle stimulation (esp. zygomatic branch)

---

#### Posterior tibial nerve

• Location: posterior to medial malleolus  
• Muscle: **flexor hallucis brevis** → great toe plantar flexion  

• Electrode placement:
  • Distal (−): just distal to ankle  
  • Proximal (+): proximal to ankle  

---

### Preparation

• Avoid:
  • Oedematous areas (↓ conduction)  
  • Sites with lines, dressings, injury  

• Skin preparation:
  • Clip hair  
  • Clean with alcohol  
  • Use gel electrodes  

• Electrode spacing: ≥**2.5cm**  

• Device:
  • Check battery  
  • Ensure correct polarity (− distal → depolarisation)  

• Correct electrolyte abnormalities:
  • ↓K⁺, ↓Ca²⁺, ↑Mg²⁺ alter response  

---

### Supramaximal stimulation

• Identifying ensures consistent nerve activation despite physiological variation over time.

Definitions:

• Threshold: minimum current → visible twitch  
• Submaximal: increasing twitch with ↑ current  
• Maximal: full contraction from current
• **Supramaximal**: Observed as no further ↑ in twitch with ↑ current. Ensure it's ~**20% above maximal**.

Technique:

1. Start low current  
2. Increase in steps (5–10mA)  
3. Identify point where twitch no longer increases  
4. Confirm that the level from step 3 is about 20% higher than the previous tested level.

> [!important]
> Determine BEFORE NMBA administration

---

### Twitch Response Interpretation

| TOF response      | Approx receptor block | Interpretation      |
| ----------------- | --------------------- | ------------------- |
| 4/4, equal        | <70%                  | No or minimal block |
| 4/4, fade present | >70%                  | Partial block       |
| 3/4               | ~75–80%               | Moderate block      |
| 2/4               | ~80%                  | Target range        |
| 1/4               | ~90%                  | Deep block          |
| 0/4               | ~95%                  | Profound block      |

**Fade (T4 < T1)**
- ==Seen with non-depolarising agents== (e.g. rocuronium, vecuronium)
- Caused by progressive ACh depletion with each stimulus — the drug wins the competition more easily as the nerve runs low
- Appears at >70% receptor blockade; as block deepens further, twitches drop out and fade becomes less relevant
- Useful clinical marker that a meaningful non-depolarising block is present even when all four twitches are still visible

**No fade — uniform depression**
- ==Seen with depolarising agents== (e.g. suxamethonium, phase 1 block)
- All four twitches equally depressed regardless of depth — T1 looks the same as T4
- Dose-dependent: low dose → 4/4 weak but equal; increasing dose → twitches drop out equally; full dose → 0/4
- Note: very large or repeated suxamethonium doses can cause phase 2 block, which mimics a non-depolarising pattern and may produce fade

---

### Target

• Typical goal: **2/4 twitches** (~80% blockade)  
• Acceptable ranges:
  • 1–2 or 2–3 depending on indication  

---

### Dose adjustment

• Too many twitches → ↑ NMBA dose  
• Too few twitches → ↓ NMBA dose  

---

### Monitoring frequency

• Initially: **hourly**  
• After dose change/bolus: every **15min**  
• Stable patient: up to **4-hourly**  

---

### Troubleshooting absent twitches (0/4)

• Check:
  • Lead connections  
  • Battery  
  • Electrode contact/skin prep  

• Consider:
  • Oedema → ↑ current required  
  • Re-site electrodes or change nerve  

• Confirm at alternative site  

> [!warning]
> Do not assume full paralysis without excluding technical error → risk of underdosing NMBA reduction  

---

### Complications / limitations

• ICU-acquired weakness with excessive blockade  
• Reduced reliability in oedema/anasarca  
• Direct muscle stimulation → false interpretation  
• Electrolyte disturbances alter response  

---

### Practical points

> [!tip]
> • Use same site consistently for trending  
> • Ulnar nerve preferred for reliability  
> • Mark electrode sites if repeated assessments required  
> • Consider objective monitors (acceleromyography) where available
