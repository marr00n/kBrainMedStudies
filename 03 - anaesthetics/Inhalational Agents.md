## Definition
Volatile anaesthetics are gases used to induce a or maintain a state of anaesthesia.

Some examples are desfluorine, isoflurine, and sevoflurine and nitrous oxide.

## Concentration and Delivery

The concentration of the gas is expressed as a percentage of its volume to the total volume of the gas mixture.

Modern anaesthetic machines are able to precisely vaporise the liquid inhalational agent in order to deliver very accurate concentrations to the patient.

## Mechanism of Action

Not fully known, likely multifactorial.

Volume Expansion Theory
- Gas dissolves in lipid cell membranes, distorting them, and impacting sodium channels needed for synapse transmission

Neurotransmitter interference
- Acts at pre-synaptic and post-synaptic receptors interfering with the release and reuptake of various neurotransmitters thereby altering synapse transmission

Activation of [GABA](GABA.md)
- Activate GABA receptors inhibiting the calcium channel release and reduce neurotransmission

## MAC

The MAC value of an inhalational agent is the alveolar concentration in oxygen at one atmosphere, that will prevent 50% of subjects from making a purposeful movement in response to a standard surgical stimulus, such as a surgical incision.

- MAC 1 = 50% of The population is anaesthetised.
- Mac 1.3 = 97.5% of the population is anaesthetised.
### Factors affecting MAC

| Increases MAC                                                             | Decreases MAC                                                                                 |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Hyperthermia<br>Chronic Drug Abuse<br>Sympathomimetics<br>Hyperthyroidism | Age<br>Hypothermia<br>Hypotension<br>Opioids<br>Benzos<br>Acute Alcohol Use<br>Hypothyroidism |
*MAC measures how hard it is to depress CNS activity. Each of these lead to a state of CNS depression or excitability and therefore determine how much agent (measured in MAC) is required to anaesthetise these patients.*

### Getting to MAC 1.3

How quickly the patient is anaethethised depends on how quickly the inhalational agent reaches the alveoli, and then how quickly the  alveoli concentration equilibrates with the brain.

Factors affecting how quickly the inhalational agent reaches the alveoli:
1. The inspired concentration of gas.
2. The gas flow rate through the machine.
3. The alveolar ventilation (respiratory rate x tidal volume).

Factors that determine how quickly the alveolar equilibrates with the brain include:
1. The rate of blood flow to the brain.
2. The solubility of the inhalational agent.
3. The difference in the arterial and venous concentrations of the inhalational agent.

#### The anaesthetic tension cascade.
> [!FAQ]
> The word <u>tension</u> is a historical term referring to the partial pressure.

The full concentration or partial pressure of the anesthetic agent is provided by the machine. Theoretically it could be diluted by exhaled gas, however most circuit settings will clear this away, meaning that the inspired concentration is closer to the concentration provided by the machine.
Because the blood is absorbing the gas, the alveolar concentration will remain below that of what the machine is providing, i.e. the inspired concentration. However, the brain will equilibrate with the alveolar concentration within 8 to 10 minutes of any changes. This means that the exhaled or the entitle anesthetic agent concentration is a good estimate of the brain concentration.
#### Cardiac output is inversely related to the depth of anaesthesia achieved.
Low cardiac output → less blood draining the alveolus → higher F<sub>A</sub> → higher arterial partial pressure → higher partial pressure gradient into the brain → deeper anaesthesia than expected at any given vaporiser setting.

Then the vicious cycle: deeper anaesthesia → more cardiorespiratory depression (volatiles are negative inotropes and vasodilators) → even lower cardiac output → even higher F<sub>A</sub> → deeper still.


### Comparing the potency of different inhalational agents.

This is measured by the **F<sub>A</sub>/F<sub>I</sub> ratio**, where:
- F<sub>Inspired</sub> = constant (what gas conc the machine delivers)
- F<sub>Alevolar</sub> = variable (what actually builds up in the alveolus)
	- F<sub>A</sub> tracks the brain concentration as we assume:
		- Alveolus conc ⇌ Arterial blood conc ⇌ Brain conc. as there's very rapid equilibration between them.

Interpreting the F<sub>A</sub>/F<sub>I</sub> ratio graph:
- Soluble gas → blood drains the alveolus → F<sub>A</sub> stays low → low ratio → low curve
- Insoluble gas → blood leaves it alone → F<sub>A</sub> builds up → high ratio → high curve
