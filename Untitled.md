# Inhaled Anaesthetic Pharmacokinetics — First Principles

The clinical effect of an inhaled agent is determined by its partial pressure (PP) in the CNS. Understanding uptake and distribution requires grounding in the physical chemistry of gases and solutions. The following principles underpin all inhaled anaesthetic pharmacokinetics.

---

## Physical Foundations

### Pressure and Partial Pressure

• Pressure is generated by molecules in constant random motion colliding with surfaces — more molecules or faster molecules → ↑ pressure. • In a gas mixture, each gas contributes independently to total pressure in proportion to its fractional concentration. This is **Dalton's Law**. • PP of a gas = fractional concentration × total pressure • At sea level (Patm ~100kPa): 2% sevoflurane exerts ~2kPa partial pressure • Gases in a mixture are mutually indifferent — each behaves as if alone in the container. No competition for "space".

### Dissolving at a Gas–Liquid Interface

• At any gas–liquid interface, gas molecules continuously enter and escape the liquid. At **equilibrium**, the rate of entry = rate of escape. • At equilibrium, the PP of the gas **above** the liquid equals the PP of that gas **within** the liquid — even if concentrations differ markedly between the two phases. • This is the key principle: **PP equalises across phases at equilibrium; concentration does not.**

### Henry's Law

• At constant temperature, the concentration of a dissolved gas in a liquid is directly proportional to its PP above the liquid. • Concentration (dissolved) = Henry's Law constant × PP • The Henry's Law constant is specific to each gas–liquid pair and varies with temperature. • **Solubility** and the Henry's Law constant are expressions of the same property — the capacity of a liquid to hold a gas at a given PP. • High solubility = high Henry's Law constant = large amount dissolved per unit PP • Low solubility = low constant = little dissolved per unit PP

### Partition Coefficients

• The **partition coefficient** between two phases is the ratio of concentrations at equilibrium (equal PP), for equal volumes of each phase. • The **blood:gas partition coefficient** answers: at equilibrium, how many times more agent is in blood than in alveolar gas? • High coefficient = high solubility in blood = ↑ agent dissolved per unit PP → PP in blood rises slowly • Low coefficient = low solubility = ↓ agent needed to raise PP → blood PP rises rapidly • Partition coefficients are analogous to **thermal mass (specific heat capacity)**: • A high-solubility medium absorbs large amounts of agent before PP rises — like a substance with high thermal mass absorbing heat before its temperature rises. • PP is analogous to temperature; agent molecules are analogous to heat energy.

### Why Partial Pressure — Not Concentration — Drives Diffusion

• Diffusion across membranes is driven by the **PP gradient**, not the concentration gradient (**Fick's Law**). • A membrane separating two compartments does not "count molecules" — it responds to the thermodynamic drive to equalise PP on both sides. • Net movement of gas continues until PP is equal on both sides, regardless of whether concentrations are equal. • Two compartments may be at equilibrium (equal PP) with very different concentrations, because their solubilities for the agent differ. • The brain equilibrates to the PP presented by arterial blood. It cannot detect the vaporiser setting — it only "sees" the PP signal the blood carries to it.

## Applied Pharmacokinetics of Inhaled Agents

### The FA/FI Ratio

• **FA** = alveolar fraction (concentration) of agent. **FI** = inspired fraction. • FA is directly proportional to alveolar PP at constant Patm — FA is effectively a proxy for alveolar PP. • **FA/FI** tracks the rate of rise of alveolar PP toward inspired PP. FA/FI = 1 = full equilibration. • Speed of induction correlates with how quickly FA/FI rises.

### Step 1 — Alveolus to Blood

• Blood flowing through pulmonary capillaries is exposed to alveolar gas and absorbs agent until PP equalises — or until that blood moves on. • How faithfully blood transmits the alveolar PP signal depends on solubility: • **Low blood:gas coefficient** (e.g. desflurane 0.45, sevoflurane 0.65): blood has ↓ capacity → few molecules needed to raise blood PP to match alveolus → rapid equilibration → blood leaving the alveolus carries close to full alveolar PP to the brain → **fast induction** • **High blood:gas coefficient** (e.g. halothane 2.4): blood has ↑ capacity → many molecules dissolve before blood PP rises → slow equilibration → blood leaves alveolus at a PP well below alveolar PP → attenuated signal to brain → **slow induction** • A high-solubility agent also continuously drains the alveolus of molecules, suppressing FA and slowing the FA/FI rise.

### Step 2 — Alveolar-Venous Partial Pressure Gradient (PA − Pv)

• Venous blood returning to the lungs carries agent at a PP determined by tissue uptake. • At the start of induction, tissues contain zero agent → maximal extraction from blood → venous PP is low → large PA − Pv gradient → blood avidly absorbs agent from alveolus → FA is suppressed → slow rise of FA/FI. • As tissues saturate, venous PP rises → gradient narrows → less uptake per alveolar pass → FA rises more freely → FA/FI climbs toward 1. • **Large AV gradient = high whole-body uptake = slow induction. Narrow AV gradient = tissues saturating = faster FA/FI rise.**

### Step 3 — Blood to Tissues (Three-Compartment Model)

Tissues are grouped by equilibration rate, determined by perfusion and solubility:

|Compartment|Tissues|Equilibration|Clinical relevance|
|---|---|---|---|
|Central (vessel-rich group)|Brain, heart, kidneys, liver|Minutes|Effect site; determines depth of anaesthesia|
|Peripheral 1 (muscle)|Skeletal muscle|Hours|Moderate sink; relevant during maintenance|
|Peripheral 2 (fat)|Adipose tissue|Days|Large reservoir for soluble agents; ↑ in prolonged cases|

• At induction, all compartments are avid sinks. The vessel-rich group (VRG) equilibrates first — once brain PP = arterial PP, the brain stops extracting agent. • Muscle then dominates uptake, followed at a distance by fat. • Progressive saturation of compartments → narrowing AV gradient → ↑ FA/FI over time. • On emergence: agent redistributes from tissues back to blood and is exhaled. After short cases, muscle and fat have accumulated little → rapid washout. After long cases or with soluble agents, tissue reservoirs slow emergence.

### Cardiac Output Effects

• ↑ CO → ↑ pulmonary blood flow → ↑ uptake from alveolus → FA suppressed → ↓ FA/FI rise → **slower induction** • ↓ CO (e.g. shock, cardiac failure) → ↓ alveolar drainage → FA rises faster → deeper anaesthesia than expected at a given vaporiser setting → **risk of overdose with soluble agents** (see [[Low Cardiac Output]]) • CO effect is more pronounced for soluble agents; insoluble agents are relatively unaffected.

### Second Gas Effect

• When N2O is delivered at high concentration (~60–70%), its rapid bulk absorption shrinks total alveolar volume. • As alveolar volume contracts at constant Patm, the fractional concentration — and therefore PP — of co-administered agents (e.g. sevoflurane) rises. • Augmented inflow of fresh gas from dead space further replenishes alveolar agent concentration. • **Net effect: ↑ FA and ↑ PP of the second agent → faster uptake and faster induction.** • N2O is uniquely suited to produce this effect: delivered at very high concentration and rapidly absorbed owing to its low solubility (blood:gas 0.47) — low solubility means rapid equilibration per unit of blood, and high inspired fraction means continuous high-volume delivery. • **Diffusion hypoxia** at emergence is the same mechanism in reverse: N2O exits blood into alveolus in bulk → dilutes alveolar O2 → ↓ PAO2. Prevent with 100% O2 at emergence.

### MAC Additivity vs Partial Pressure Additivity

• **Dalton's Law additivity** (physical): each gas in a mixture exerts its own PP independently. PPs do not combine into a single meaningful sum. • **MAC additivity** (pharmacodynamic): 0.5 MAC N2O + 0.5 MAC sevoflurane = 1 MAC of CNS effect. This is a biological observation — the brain integrates the separate PP-driven effects of each agent. • These are additive at different levels: Dalton's Law operates at the physical level; MAC additivity operates at the receptor/CNS level.
